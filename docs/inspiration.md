# WAKE ALONE -- source bible (public-domain inspiration)

Legally-safe wells to draw the "haunted spaceship" feeling from, with craft takeaways per
source. Built from a verified research pass (citations at the bottom). The point is to inherit
the DNA of *Alien*, *Alien: Isolation*, and *Dead Space* without touching their (copyrighted)
bodies -- by going to the public-domain ancestors those works themselves grew from.

## The one rule

**Inspiration and motifs only. No verbatim copying. No named invented IP.** Ideas, premises,
genre tropes, and historical facts are free. Specific wording and named invented elements are
not. Never use another work's text, and never use named entities like Cthulhu, the xenomorph,
or the necromorph -- invent your own. Re-author everything in the house voice (sparse second
person, present-leaning), which also keeps the prose linter happy.

## Distilled craft rules (read these first)

The actionable distillation for authoring and generation. Each traces to a verified source below.

1. **Never describe the presence directly.** Reveal it only by indirect signs and displacement:
   a sound that stops the instant you stop, a thing moved an inch, rations gone, a hatch you
   know you closed now open. To name it is to lessen it. (*The Willows*)
2. **The horror is attention.** Tie sanity loss to being noticed; make stillness and not-looking
   the safest moves. The thing senses minds, not eyes. (*The Willows*)
3. **Open mundane, then strip the ordinary.** Start in banal ship routine and remove normalcy
   one beat at a time until the familiar itself feels wrong. (*The Willows*)
4. **Atmosphere over event, suggestion over exposition.** Build dread by imperceptible hints and
   selective associative detail, not catalogues of happenings. (Lovecraft's craft doctrine)
5. **Charge ordinary objects with dread** -- a chair, a coolant drip, a crew log -- rather than
   inventing a monster to point at. (Blackwood)
6. **The ship is a thin shell against an immense hostile dark.** Ground the uncanny in real,
   physical mechanical detail so the wrongness lands. (Hodgson)
7. **Use the becalming:** power dead, systems drifting, no way to move; the crew lost one by one;
   the survivor's own guilt as the engine. (*Rime of the Ancient Mariner*)
8. **The empty-but-recently-occupied scene:** belongings undisturbed, a half-eaten meal, no
   bodies, no note. The dread of absence. (Mary Celeste)
9. **Body horror as wrong-organic design, not splatter** -- biological forms turned subtly
   inhuman. (see Haeckel, pending)
10. **Keep the ending earned and the world indifferent.** No rescue arrives because it cares;
    the cosmos does not notice you. (cosmic-horror DNA)

## Legal frame (verified -- read before using anything)

- **US public domain is by publication date, not author death.** The wall as of 2026: published
  **1930 or earlier = public domain** in the US. Two intuitive shortcuts were explicitly refuted
  in research and must NOT be used: "author died >70 years ago, so it's PD" (false for US), and
  "died in 1918, so all works PD" (false -- use the confirmed publication dates).
- **Lovecraft is a legal minefield -- use the doctrine, not the text.** His famous post-1923
  stories are NOT confirmed public domain, and his named Mythos entities are not safe (Chaosium
  disclaims owning the Mythos *as a whole* but claims specific named elements). Draw the cosmic
  DNA from Blackwood, Hodgson, and Coleridge instead. Lovecraft's *craft principles* are fine to
  follow (ideas are uncopyrightable); his *prose* is not free to quote at length.
- **Translations carry their own copyright.** For non-English sources (e.g. Maupassant), the
  original may be PD while a modern English translation is not. Use a pre-1930 translation or
  paraphrase the idea.
- **Sound recordings are generally NOT public domain by age**, even when the underlying
  composition is. For audio, use NASA-created or CC0 sources, not vintage recordings.
- **NASA media** is generally not copyright-restricted in the US and is good for reference
  imagery and audio -- but avoid the NASA insignia/logo/seal, and skip any asset marked with a
  third-party copyright holder.
- **Public-domain art on Wikimedia Commons:** rely on the artwork's PD-by-age status, not the
  page-footer CC license text (which covers page metadata, not the centuries-old work).

## Franchise -> public-domain ancestor map

The franchise tropes (small crew, isolation, an unseen stalker on an enclosed vessel, existential
dread) are uncopyrightable genre conventions. Target the same DNA through these PD roots:

| Vibe target | What you actually want | Draw it from |
|---|---|---|
| *Alien* | derelict ship, unseen stalker, crew picked off, corporate indifference | Hodgson (besieged vessel) + *The Willows* (unseen presence) + *Rime* (doomed, becalmed ship) |
| *Alien: Isolation* | it hunts you; hide, stay unseen, don't be detected | **The Willows** -- "keep perfectly still," "it's our minds they feel," "don't think." The cleanest map. |
| *Dead Space* (minus the action) | derelict mining vessel, body horror, a maddening artifact, creeping insanity | Lovecraftian contamination DNA via PD ancestors + *Rime* (guilt/madness) + Mary Celeste (derelict) + Haeckel (wrong-organic forms) |

## Verified core sources

### The Willows -- Algernon Blackwood (1907)  [PD: confirmed]
The single most on-target source; Lovecraft ranked it the foremost weird tale.
- **Style:** the menace is never directly described -- only inferred from stirred foliage, a slit
  canoe, funnel-shaped hollows, drained supplies. Ordinary objects charged with horror stimulate
  the imagination more than monsters. "To name is to reveal."
- **Arc:** opens as a genteel travelogue and progressively strips the quotidian until reality
  itself is besieged; cumulative escalation to doom.
- **Elements:** an unseen presence that senses minds; survival by keeping still and not thinking;
  a victim taken; two-person isolation in a place that is itself the antagonist.
- **Atmosphere:** make the environment patiently hostile; signal wrongness through small
  displacements, not set-pieces. **This is the literal model for the sanity/attention mechanic.**

### William Hope Hodgson -- sea & cosmic horror (1907-1912)  [PD: confirmed by publication date]
*The Boats of the "Glen Carrig"* (1907), *The House on the Borderland* (1908), *The Ghost
Pirates* (1909), *The Night Land* (1912), and the Sargasso Sea stories. (He was a sailor for ~8
years; the maritime detail is authentic.)
- **Style:** concrete, physical, claustrophobic; real mechanical/nautical detail grounds the
  uncanny.
- **Arc:** a vessel besieged from outside the hull -- a siege that tightens.
- **Elements:** the derelict / sealed vessel, things in the dark beyond the hull, a crew worn
  down, a malign outside. The Sargasso "graveyard of derelicts" = a ship-graveyard debris field
  in space.
- **Atmosphere:** the ship as a fragile shell against an immense hostile dark.

### The Rime of the Ancient Mariner -- Coleridge (1798), with Gustave Dore's engravings (c. 1876)  [PD: confirmed]
- **Arc (a ready-made template):** a becalmed, immobilized vessel ("Day after day ... We stuck,
  nor breath nor motion"); the crew die one by one, staring in reproach; a lone survivor narrates;
  the whole calamity flows from a single act. Isolation -> escalation -> doom -> haunted survivor.
- **Elements:** the curse you brought on yourself (player guilt), the dead crew, powerlessness,
  penance.
- **Art direction:** Dore's 53-plate cycle -- ship trapped in ice, "So lonely," the mariner
  watching the sea -- is high-contrast monochrome that suits the CRT/phosphor look. Use the
  engravings as PD-by-age; ignore the Commons page license text.

### The Mary Celeste (1872)  [PD: historical facts]
A real merchant brigantine found adrift and deserted off the Azores, seaworthy, cargo and
belongings undisturbed, no one ever seen again. The literal "wake alone, everyone gone, no
explanation." Use the **premise** (facts are uncopyrightable), not any author's retelling.
Realistic, usable details: water in the hold, a missing lifeboat and navigation instruments --
i.e. a missing escape pod, a slow leak.

### Lovecraft -- craft doctrine ONLY (not his text)  [text NOT safe; principles are free]
- "Atmosphere, not action, is the great desideratum of weird fiction."
- Build dread through "subtle suggestion -- imperceptible hints and touches of selective
  associative detail," not exposition or "bald catalogues of incredible happenings."
- **Do not** quote his prose at length (the essay is 1937; stories post-1923 unconfirmed) or use
  named Mythos entities. Follow the method; invent your own cosmos.

### NASA media  [PD: confirmed, with caveats]
Generally not copyright-restricted in the US (17 U.S.C. 105). Best use: reference imagery and --
crucially -- **audio**, since vintage sound recordings are not PD by age. Caveats: avoid the
insignia/logo/seal; skip any asset marked with a third-party copyright holder.

## Strong candidates -- pending a publication-date check

Named in scope but NOT independently verified in this pass. Each is very likely PD by US
publication date, but confirm the date (and, for translations, the translator) before relying.

- **Edgar Allan Poe** (d. 1849; works PD): confinement and atmosphere -- *The Fall of the House
  of Usher*, *MS. Found in a Bottle*, *A Descent into the Maelstrom*, *The Pit and the Pendulum*.
- **Charlotte Perkins Gilman, "The Yellow Wallpaper" (1892):** sanity erosion in an enclosing
  room that seems to watch -- a direct model for degrading, sanity-keyed narration.
- **Robert W. Chambers, "The King in Yellow" (1895):** a maddening artifact; madness as contagion.
- **Guy de Maupassant, "The Horla" (1887):** an unseen watcher, paranoia, a mind unravelling.
  *Translation caveat applies.*
- **Arthur Machen, "The Great God Pan" (1894):** cosmic dread, the uncanny behind the veil.
- **Ambrose Bierce:** "The Damned Thing" (an invisible creature), "An Occurrence at Owl Creek
  Bridge" (unreliable, collapsing reality).
- **M.R. James:** ghost stories of slow accumulating dread; collections span pre- and post-1930 --
  check each story's date.
- **Visual art:** Piranesi's *Carceri* (impossible labyrinthine architecture -- the ship as maze),
  Caspar David Friedrich (*The Sea of Ice* -- a vessel crushed by an indifferent sublime), Ernst
  Haeckel's *Kunstformen der Natur* (radiolaria/jellyfish -- alien-biology design language), Goya's
  Black Paintings, Odilon Redon's noirs.
- **Real events:** the Franklin Expedition (1845) and Shackleton's *Endurance* -- doomed/marooned
  crews in a hostile void. Facts are PD; specific expedition photographs need a date/source check.
