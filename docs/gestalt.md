# Gestalt: evoke, do not catalogue (the default prose mode)

The house default for WAKE ALONE prose: **give the reader a few charged, specific fragments and let
their own mind assemble the whole.** The picture they paint is more frightening, and more theirs,
than anything you spell out. This is not a special effect for unfamiliar objects (that is lesson L4,
the extreme case) - it is how every room, body, and presence should be written.

## Why it works (four frames that say the same thing)

- **Gestalt closure (perception).** The mind completes incomplete forms on its own, filling gaps
  from prior experience - given "only enough information to hint towards what is missing." A few
  fragments read as a whole. ([gestalt law of closure](https://helpfulprofessor.com/gestalt-law-of-closure/))
- **The iceberg / theory of omission (Hemingway).** "If a writer ... knows enough of what he is
  writing about he may omit things ... and the reader ... will have a feeling of those things as
  strongly as though the writer had stated them." One-eighth shows; the weight is the seven-eighths
  below. Crucial caveat: "a writer who omits things because he does not know them only makes hollow
  places in his writing." ([iceberg theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceberg_theory))
- **Gaps and blanks (Iser, reader-response).** A text is "instructions"; the reader fills the
  "unwritten implications" by imagination, and the story that results - the "virtual text" - is
  co-created. A mix of *determinacy* (anchors that steer) and *indeterminacy* (gaps that invite)
  conditions the whole interaction. ([Iser's gaps](https://literariness.org/2018/02/12/key-theories-of-wolfgang-iser/))
- **Horror craft.** "What we imagine is far scarier than what we actually see ... when you leave
  gaps, readers will fill them with their worst fears." Lurk in the telling detail - a familiar
  object slightly out of place - and show the threat through reaction, not exposition.
  ([writing horror descriptions](https://www.crystallakepub.com/shadows-ink-blog-writing-descriptions-in-horror-fiction/))

## How to apply it

1. **The telling detail (synecdoche).** One precise part stands for the whole. *"Ten of them are
   open. Empty."* tells the crew's fate without a word about the crew. Not a room inventory - the
   one object that implies the rest.
2. **Name the effect, not the thing.** Describe what it does to the air, the body, you - not what it
   looks like. VIGIL never describes the presence; it gives you *"the emptiness has a weight to it
   and a patience."* The horror in `derelict` is a dead face read as *"not pain, not fear.
   Attention."* - an expression, not a wound.
3. **Charge the ordinary.** An everyday object made slightly wrong carries more dread than an
   invented monster. A glove on the deck, palm up. A chair turned. (Blackwood's method.)
4. **Reaction over object.** The held breath, the refusal to look, the choice to stay still - render
   the fear and let its cause stay offstage.
5. **One controlling image, not three.** A single resonant figure - a shuttle hatch *"open like a
   mouth"* - beats a pile of competing similes. (This is also lesson L2: do not stack metaphors.)
6. **Sensation over specification.** *"Cold. The kind that has teeth."* not "minus forty."

## What gestalt is NOT (the failure modes)

- **Not vagueness.** "Something horrible happened" is a hollow place, not an evocation. Gestalt is
  *specific* fragments, precisely chosen - not a generic blur. Vagueness-from-not-knowing is exactly
  the iceberg's warning; know what is under the water even when you omit it.
- **Not withholding everything.** The reader needs enough determinate anchors to close the gap the
  way you intend. Too few, and they cannot assemble it (that is the muddy-ending problem in L2).
  Determinacy guides; indeterminacy invites - you need both.
- **Not purple.** More adjectives is the opposite of gestalt. Cut, then cut again; the gap does the
  work.

## How it relates to the other rules

- It generalizes **L4** (out-of-domain items): gestalt is the default everywhere, and an object
  outside the protagonist's expertise pushes it to the maximum - gestalt only, no function, no hints.
- It serves **L2**: gestalt the *image*, never the *logic*. The causal chain of an ending stays
  determinate and legible; only the imagery stays suggestive.
- It is the prose engine behind the bible's "dread over gore" and "the horror is attention."

## Specimens from the prose we analyzed

- **The Yellow Wallpaper** (Gilman): the thing behind the wallpaper is never shown whole - only
  *"two bulbous eyes stare at you upside-down"*, then *"unblinking eyes are everywhere"*, a shape
  that "sulks about behind" the pattern. The reader assembles the figure from eyes and motion.
- **The Rime of the Ancient Mariner** (Coleridge): the dead becalming is one image, not a log -
  *"As idle as a painted ship"*; *"Water, water, every where, / Nor any drop to drink."*
- **The Willows** (Blackwood): the menace exists only as indirect signs - a slit canoe, funnel-shaped
  hollows, drained provisions, "vague shapes or presences." "To name is to reveal," so it is never
  named.
- **Our own**: `derelict`'s *"not pain, not fear. Attention."*; VIGIL's emptiness with *"a weight
  to it and a patience"*; TENANT's *"a glove lies on the deck by the hatch, palm up"* and the hatch
  *"open like a mouth."*

These are studied for technique, never copied (see `corpus/SOURCES.md`).
